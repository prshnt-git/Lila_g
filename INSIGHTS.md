# INSIGHTS

## Insight 1: Combat concentrates into repeat hotspots instead of spreading evenly

### What caught my eye
Kill activity repeatedly formed dense clusters rather than being evenly distributed across the map.

### Evidence
The kill heatmap showed a small number of clearly stronger hotspots, suggesting repeated route convergence into the same contested spaces.

### Actionable takeaway
This suggests some routes or points of interest are over-funneling players into the same combat spaces.

### Metrics likely affected
- combat encounter rate
- route diversity
- survival duration
- spatial spread of player movement

### Why a level designer should care
If too much combat happens in too few places, the map can feel repetitive and reduce meaningful route choice.

---

## Insight 2: Loot-heavy areas do not always overlap with combat-heavy areas

### What caught my eye
Some areas showed strong loot activity without equally strong combat pressure.

### Evidence
The loot heatmap highlighted farming regions that did not fully overlap with the strongest kill zones.

### Actionable takeaway
These zones may be offering too much reward for too little risk.

### Metrics likely affected
- loot collection efficiency
- early-game route choice
- PvP encounter frequency
- risk/reward balance

### Why a level designer should care
If valuable loot is too safe, players may settle into dominant farming routes and avoid intended tension points.

---

## Insight 3: Storm pressure is more localized but still meaningful

### What caught my eye
Storm-death patterns appeared in smaller, more isolated clusters than combat pressure.

### Evidence
Storm heatmaps were not broadly distributed. Instead, they appeared around specific route or edge areas, suggesting targeted pacing or traversal pressure.

### Actionable takeaway
This may point to route readability issues, pacing problems, or areas where players are getting trapped by environmental pressure.

### Metrics likely affected
- storm death rate
- late-phase survival
- route completion rate
- frustration on specific edge paths

### Why a level designer should care
Storm deaths often indicate map pacing and route clarity problems, not just combat skill differences.

---

## Final note

These insights are intended as starting points for design investigation.  
The main value of the tool is that it makes spatial gameplay patterns visible quickly enough for level designers to iterate on map flow and pressure.
